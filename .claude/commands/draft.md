description: "Composition and structure for getting a piece written. Use when asked to write, draft, or create any content longer than a paragraph. Works with /edit for revision."
---
Build the piece in stages. This skill covers the first stage: getting the content down. Do not self-edit while drafting. The /edit skill handles that.

The reader is a smart peer. Assume they can follow complex reasoning, technical vocabulary, and dense sentences. Never simplify for safety. Sometimes clarity requires a blunt, four-word sentence. Other times it requires forty words with two subordinate clauses. Match the sentence to the idea.

Before you start, name the register. Is this clinical, conversational, argumentative, explanatory, narrative? A piece about database internals and a piece about a friend's bad decision should not sound the same, even if both are written in prose. Patio11 writes about mundane business infrastructure (payment processing, SaaS metrics, compliance) and makes it compelling because he matches the register to the stakes: deadpan when describing how a system works, sharp when describing how it fails people. The register should come from the subject, not from a house style applied uniformly.

Start with something the reader can hold. The first sentence should do one of three things: state a concrete fact that raises a question, make a claim the reader will want to test, or drop them into a scene they can see. "In 2019, a startup called Levels shipped a CSV export feature that accidentally included every customer's email address" does all three. "In this essay, I will discuss data security" does none of them. Never open with a meta-description of what the piece will cover. Begin inside the subject.

Prose is the default. A list justifies itself only when three or more items carry genuine parallel structure the reader needs to scan or compare. Go deep on a few points rather than surveying a dozen at shallow depth. What would patio11 do? He would spend 2,000 words on how a single CSV export accidentally exposed customer data, rather than 200 words each on ten different security topics.

Ground every abstraction in a concrete example within two sentences. If you write "incentive misalignment causes organizational dysfunction," the next sentence should describe a specific team, a specific decision, and what went wrong. The abstraction tells the reader what to think. The example lets them see whether they agree. Without the example, the abstraction is an assertion the reader has to take on faith.

Questions turn corners and open new lines of thought. They should never function as softeners for claims you could make outright. "Does the team actually need a standup?" is a transition. "Might one perhaps consider whether the standup is suboptimal?" is cowardice in a question costume.

Anticipate where the reader will push back and address the objection inside the argument, not in a separate section. If you write "remote teams outperform colocated teams," the reader who manages an office is already composing a rebuttal. Give them the strongest version of their objection in the next sentence or two, then explain why you think the claim holds anyway (or concede where it doesn't). Patio11 does this constantly: he states something provocative, immediately steelmans the obvious counterargument, and then explains why the original point survives it.

Where evidence is thin or a claim rests on a single study or your own experience, say so. Real analysis has loose ends. Show them. Patio11 routinely says things like "I am not a lawyer, but I have been sued, and here is what I learned from that." The qualification and the authority arrive in the same sentence.

Use direct quotes when the original language is better than your paraphrase, when the exact wording matters for the argument, or when attributing a specific position to a specific person. Paraphrase when you need to compress, when the original is jargon-heavy and the reader needs a translation, or when you're synthesizing across multiple sources. Summarize when the source's conclusion matters but the reasoning behind it doesn't. Default to paraphrase. A piece that over-quotes reads like a collage. A piece that never quotes feels like everything was filtered through one voice.

When you compare two positions, give them unequal space. Let the stronger side take three sentences and the weaker side take one. Or concede the weaker side's best point inside the argument for the stronger side. Perfect symmetry between two positions signals that the writer mapped the structure before thinking about the content.

Between sections, you have three options. A question that sets up the next topic. A sentence that connects the previous section's conclusion to the next section's premise. Or nothing at all: just start the new section cold. The third option works more often than most writers think. If the sections are in the right order, the reader makes the connection themselves. Do not write transition paragraphs that exist only to announce what comes next.

Headers work as questions or short provocations. Use them sparingly. A piece needing a header every three paragraphs has a problem no header can fix.

Calibrate length before you start. What is the minimum number of paragraphs that covers this topic without leaving gaps? Write that many. If the next paragraph does not add something the reader would miss, cut it. A 200-word answer to a 200-word question is fine. A 2,000-word answer to the same question needs to justify the extra 1,800 words with new information in each paragraph. Paul Graham is good at this: his essays feel long because they cover ground, not because they pad.
